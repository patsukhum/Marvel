In fact, setting aside some notable exceptions like Avatar, it seems as though almost all the biggest movies these days are action-packed comic book stories adapted for the big screen. Let’s look at some data to see if this is really the case:

Marvel vs. DC Comics

Without a doubt, the most dominant franchises in the comic book movie genre are Marvel and DC. Both the Marvel Cinematic Universe (MCU) and DC Extended Universe (DCEU) have been churning out huge numbers of successful (and not so successful) movies, ever since Iron Man came out in 2008. Both stayed neck-and-neck in terms of yearly box office revenues until the mid 2010’s, after MCU’s [MOVIE] came out. After that, it’s simply no contest: MCU is the dominant comic book movie franchise (sorry DC fans).

The Marvel Cinematic Universe

One of the defining features (one might say, 'superpowers') of the MCU is the sheer number of characters and stories that have been woven into its single timeline. Beginning with Iron Man in 2008, MCU began producing movies with increasing frequency and increasing success. Now in 2019, there have been a total of [NUMBER] MCU movies from [NUMBER] different series, with plans for [NUMBER] more.

However, a linear timeline hides the strength of MCU: the complexity of how the movies and characters interact with one another. The story of MCU is not linear, but is highly complex and branching. Marvel Character Networks

And the complexity doesn’t end at the movie level! The relations between each of the many main characters in MCU are highly complex. To get a sense of this, we downloaded the Wikipedia pages of each MCU main character using the Wikimedia API and extracted the links between character pages. Each link feasibly represents some sort of relation between the two characters. For example, if Hawkeye links to Thor, then somewhere on Hawkeye’s page is a reference to Thor, meaning that the two characters did something together.

We can analyze this graph to determine who the most “central” or “important” character is in the MCU[1]. Based on Wikipedia links the most important character is… [drumroll please]

Captain America!

Marvel Characters: Who is the strongest?

Of course, the relationships between characters are only part of the story. These are superheros after all - what about their powers? We can look at two kinds of data: abilities (quantitative traits such as speed, strength, etc) and powers (specific superpowers such as healing, flight, etc.). We’ve also included columns for if their powers were acquired or innate, as well as one for gender (red means male) Looking down the columns, we can see that super strength, speed, intelligence, and flight are much more common than, say, healing or magical powers. It seems as though the characters with the largest influence - Captain America, Iron Man, Thor – tend to also have a similar set of powers – super-strength, speed, intelligence – as well as a distinctive weapon and perhaps flight. On the other hand, characters with lower influence tend to be equipped with a different suite of powers and abilities.

The future of the MCU?

After Endgame, it’s hard to imagine where the MCU might go next. Yet, many more movies are in the works - the so-called “Phase 4” of the MCU - including Black Widow, Spider-Man III, Black Panther II, and many more. With this next set of movies, only one thing is certain: comic book movies and the MCU are not going anywhere.
